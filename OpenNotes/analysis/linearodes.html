Linear ODEs

Linear Ordinary Differential Equations are equations that take the form:

\[a_n(x)\frac{d^ny}{dx^n} + a_{n-1}(x)\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}} + ... + a_1(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + a_0(x)y = f(x)\]

To solve the equation, we need two functions:

  • The complimentary function, \(y_c(x) = c_1y_1(x) + c_2y_2(x) + ... + c_ny_n(x)\)
  • The particular integral, \(y_p(x)\), which is any equation that satisfies the ODE directly so long as it is linearly independent of \(y_c(x)\)

In the case that \(f(x) = 0\), the general solution of the linear ODE is the complimentary function.

Boundary conditions are used to determine the unknown constants in the complimentary function, but these must be applied after the particular integral is determined.

Linear ODE with constant coefficients

To find the complimentary function, standard practice is to assume a solution in the form of \(y = Ae^{\lambda x}\), substitue that into the ODE, and divide through by \(Ae^{\lambda x}\) to get the auxillary equation in \(\lambda\).

If all n roots of the auxillary equation are all real and distinct:

Then the complimentary function takes the form

\[ \large y_c(x) = c_1e^{\lambda _1 x} + c_2e^{\lambda _2 x} + ... + c_ne^{\lambda _n x}\]

If some roots are repeated:

If a root \(\lambda _1\) occurs \(k\) times (\(k>1\)), then we must find \(k-1\) further solutions that are linearly independent of the ones already found. By substitution into the original ODE, we can show that: \[\large xe^{\lambda _1 x}, x^2e^{\lambda _1 x}, ..., x^{k-1}e^{\lambda _1 x}\] are also solutions.

Solution Method for Complimentary Function: Set RHS of ODE equal to zero, substitute \(y = Ae^{\lambda x}\) and determine a polynomial equation in \(\lambda\). Solve for the \(n\) roots to find \(\lambda\).


To find the particular integral, it is best to analyze the RHS of the ODE and attempt a parameterized version. AKA if \(f(x) = ae^x\), try \(y_p(x) = be^x\) - substituting into the ODE to determine \(b\).

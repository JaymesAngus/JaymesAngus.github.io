subsurface-habitability zones

Organisms have been found to exist at depth of up to several km under the Earth's surface in fissures and porous rocks. A paper by McMahon et al. proposed the term "subsurface-habitablility zone"[1] to described the increased range around a host star at which life on an exoplanet may survive at some depth under the planetary surface.

This concept can also be applied to icy moons harbouring liquid oceans beneath vast layers of ice, such as Jupiter's moon Europa and potentially Saturn's Mimas[2]. Our understanding of gas giants suggests that most would exhibit strong magnetospheres which could accelerate charged particles to relativistic speeds. Thick surface ice on moons orbiting these giants could provide shelter from that intense radiation.

If tidal heating or geothermal energy was sufficient to allow for liquid water, then icy moons should in theory be habitable regardless of distance from their host star.
